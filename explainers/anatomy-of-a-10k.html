The anatomy of a 10-K

The annual report runs hundreds of pages. Four sections do most of the work.

By the Filings Desk · evergreen

The 10-K is the deepest document a U.S. filer produces: an audited, comprehensive annual report with a structure fixed by regulation. Every 10-K has the same numbered items in the same order, which means you can navigate any company's report once you have learned the map.

Item 1 describes the business in the company's own words: segments, products, customers, competition. Item 1A lists risk factors, which read as boilerplate until they change. Comparing this year's risk factors against last year's is one of the oldest tricks in fundamental research, because new language usually marks a new problem.

Item 7, Management's Discussion and Analysis, is where the company explains its own numbers: why revenue moved, what drove margins, where liquidity stands. Item 8 holds the audited financial statements and the notes, and the notes carry the details that never make headlines: debt maturities, lease obligations, segment economics, legal reserves.

Foreign private issuers file a 20-F instead, and certain Canadian companies file a 40-F. The structures differ but the idea is the same: one audited annual document, everything material, on the record.

A 10-K exceeds what fits in a short note, and the digest says so plainly: long filings are truncated before drafting, every note links to the full document, and the truncation is disclosed in the pipeline itself.
