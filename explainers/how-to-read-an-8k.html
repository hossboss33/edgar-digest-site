How to read an 8-K in five minutes

Current reports are short, fast, and structured. Here is where the substance hides.

By the Filings Desk · evergreen

An 8-K is the form a U.S. public company files when something material happens between quarterly reports. The SEC requires most 8-Ks within four business days of the triggering event, which makes them the fastest official signal a company produces. Earnings ship as 8-Ks. So do executive departures, new credit agreements, acquisitions, and bylaw changes.

The form is organized by item numbers, and the item number tells you the category before you read a word. Item 2.02 is results of operations. Item 5.02 covers directors and officers arriving or leaving. Item 1.01 announces a material agreement, and Item 8.01 is the catch-all for everything else a company decides to disclose.

Start with the item number, then jump to the exhibit. The narrative section of an 8-K is often two paragraphs of lawyer prose; the substance sits in the attached exhibit, which might be a press release, the credit agreement itself, or a separation letter. The exhibit index at the bottom of the filing lists them.

Watch the dates. An 8-K discloses the event date and the filing date separately, and the gap between them is information. A resignation dated June 28 and filed July 2 used most of the four-day window.

The digest reads 8-Ks the same way you would: it quotes the filing text, checks every number against the quote, and links the original so you can read the exhibit yourself.
