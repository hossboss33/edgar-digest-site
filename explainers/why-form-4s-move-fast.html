Why insider filings move faster than the news

Directors and officers must report their trades within two business days. That clock is the point.

By the Filings Desk · evergreen

A Form 4 reports a change in beneficial ownership: a director, officer, or 10% owner bought, sold, or received securities. Since Sarbanes-Oxley in 2002, the deadline has been two business days after the transaction. Before that law, insiders had until the tenth day of the following month.

The two-day clock means Form 4s often reach EDGAR before any press release mentions the transaction, and most transactions never get a press release at all. Routine equity grants, planned sales under 10b5-1 trading plans, and small open-market purchases all flow through the same form.

Reading one takes a minute once you know the codes. Transaction code P is an open-market purchase and S is a sale. A is a grant or award from the company. M is an option exercise, and F is shares withheld to cover taxes. The form also states the price, the share count, and the holdings that remain afterward.

Context matters more than any single filing. A scheduled sale under a trading plan adopted months earlier says little. The same officer buying with cash on the open market says more. The form discloses which is which: footnotes flag 10b5-1 plans.

This digest watches Form 4s across the whole list but ranks them below annual and current reports when a day runs heavy, because most insider filings are routine. The uncovered count in each issue tells you what was left out.
